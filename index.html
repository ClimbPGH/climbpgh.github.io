ClimbPittsburgh!

Mission Statement

Climb Pittsburgh intends to bring together the appropriate stakeholders in the outdoor recreational community and the City of Pittsburgh in order to provide area residents with

  1. The opportunity to learn the fundamentals of rock climbing in order to safely practice this activity within our city limits.
  2. The access to the necessary equipment and facilities to practice this activity at zero cost to the city of Pittsburgh and zero cost to themselves.
Traditionally, rock climbing has been an activity of privilege dependent upon the ability to travel and acquire specialized gear and mentorship. Our goal is to eliminate these hurdles by installing and maintaining climbing routes within our city green-spaces, and providing interested residents and visitors with open access to these climbing areas.

Why Pittsburgh?

Growth of Rock Climbing

Sustainability of Pittsburgh

Pittsburgh's unique combination of amazing parks and green spaces allow it to be at the forefront of outdoor play.

Accessibility

Problem : Rock climbing is traditionally a sport that is an exclusive activity. It takes money , expertise, and travel.

Solution : Our mission is to jump through these hoops and bring rock climbing to the city of Pittsburgh at zero cost and in a convenient location, with full involvement from a wide variety of stakeholders.

The Plan

The act defines "recreational purpose" as "any activity undertaken or viewed for exercise, sport, educcation, recreation, relaxation or pleasure"

Phase 1: Sanctining of non-roped climbing (bouldering) in appropriate areas.

Climbers "traverse" this short wall (i.e. climb from one side to the other without ever getting very high off the ground). The site is ready to go. All that is needed is appropriate signage.

The Arena in Oakwood Park

Phase 2: Sanctioning of roped climbing along with anchor installation in appropriate areas that serve no purpose in their present state.

The abandoned pillar in Schenley Park circa Boulevard of the Allies Bridge, accessed from The Dinosaur Park across from Schenley Park Pool. This structure is 25' high and people have been climbing on it for over 30 years. Currently there is very old and unsafe hardware on this pillar. We would replace the old hardware with state of art equipment that far exceeds industry standard.


The abandoned pillar in Schenley Park.

The Mon Wharf pillar presently serves no function. It is approximately 45’ high and if equipped with proper hardware would accomodate 4-6 distinct climbing routes of moderate levels in a high profile location that would showcase nicely the unique recreational opportunities of our city.


The abandoned pillar on the Mon Wharf.

Phase 3: Sanctioning of roped climbing along with anchor installation on designated structures where we are certain we will not interfere with, nor compromise in any way, any current utility of the existing structure.

The back side of the retaining wall at Frank Curto Park next to Bigelow Blvd is approximately 30’ high and would easily accomodate a half a dozen or so routes to be equipped with bolts for leading and also top anchors.

Frank Curto Park

The walls underneath the Panther Hollow Bridge are close to 70’ high. The featured nature of the individual blocks that form these walls creates an endless variety of natural climbing holds. By placing top anchors at a suitable distance beneath the bridge, there would never be a need for climbers to be on top of the bridge nor to be recreating anywhere near the Boulevard. All activity would be contained in the park below. Given its location in the heart of one of Pittsburgh’s most beloved City Parks, we feel this would be a major destination for outdoor enthusiasts.

The walls beneath The Panther Hollow Bridge in Schenley Park.

The Seldom Seen Greenway has a several decade-long history of urban climbing in Pittsburgh and has the potential to yield the greatest number of climbing routes in the area. We have visions of transforming this space from the underutilized “secret crag” that it currently is to a shared treasure of outdoor recreation that will prove to be a destination worthy of regional travel. Much of the hardware installed in these various walls are 20-30 years old and needs to be replaced to keep things safe.Climb Pittsburgh would love to partner with the City of Pittsburgh to care for this space.

Seldom Seen Greenway.

Phase 4: Permission to install hardware and artificial climbing holds on existing structures within and around various green spaces of our city.

The walls underneath the Bloomfield Bridge in the Melwood Parklet in Polish Hill would be an ideal site for installing artificial holds. At the base of these walls is unused green spaces that we would like to transform into recreational spaces to be enjoyed by the public at large, while climbers of all levels enjoy the routes to be installed on these walls.

Melwood Parklet beneath Bloomfield Bridge.

The upward arcing nature of the sides of these walls (see yellow dashes above) would allow for installation of climbing holds on overhanging terrain to create steep overhanging routes --for many, this is the most sought after kind of climbing anywhere.
